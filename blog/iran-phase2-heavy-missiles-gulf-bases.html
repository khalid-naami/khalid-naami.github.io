Phase 2: Iran Shifts to Heavy Missiles on Gulf Bases

July 8, 2026 · 8 min read
Khalid Naami
Founder, Owner, and CEO at Dashboard Options

The conflict has entered a fundamentally different operational phase. What the American side designates as "Phase 2" involves expanded B-52, B-2 Spirit, and B-21 Raider strikes against Iran's strategic missile launch sites and underground cities. But the Iranian response has escalated in equal measure—and in a direction that Washington and Tel Aviv did not anticipate.

Iran has shifted from targeting radars, electronic warfare systems, and logistical support infrastructure to destroying the physical infrastructure of American and Israeli military bases across the entire Gulf region using heavy ballistic missiles. The rules of engagement have changed completely.

The American Phased Campaign: From Missiles to Nuclear Infrastructure​

The original American operational plan was structured in sequential phases. Phase 1 focused on neutralizing Iran's ballistic missile capability through strikes on mountain-based missile cities using B-2 Spirit and B-52 Stratofortress sorties. Phase 2, as initially conceived, was meant to shift toward destroying Iran's nuclear enrichment facilities and missile manufacturing plants.

However, the operational timeline has been compressed and resequenced. What was originally planned as Phase 2—striking nuclear infrastructure—has now been pushed to Phase 3. The current Phase 2 has been redefined as an expanded bombardment campaign targeting the strategic launch infrastructure that survived the initial wave, using the full spectrum of American strategic bombers including the B-21 Raider.

The American objective remains unchanged: force the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) to lay down its arms. President Trump has publicly called on the IRGC to surrender. The escalation to Phase 2 is designed to make that demand unavoidable by eliminating Iran's ability to project force.

Iran's Tactical Pivot: From Drones to Destruction​

In Phase 1, Iran's retaliatory strikes were deliberately calibrated. The IRGC targeted radars, electronic warfare nodes, and digital logistics systems supporting American operations—the auxiliary infrastructure that enables combat operations without striking the physical bases themselves. The intent was to degrade operational capability while preserving a degree of escalation management.

Phase 2 has changed that calculus entirely. Following the American declaration that "the initiative is in America's hands" and the direct escalation by Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, the IRGC's response has been symmetrical: if the United States escalates to destroying Iran's core infrastructure, Iran will destroy the core infrastructure of American bases.

The shift is concrete and measurable:

  • From drones to heavy ballistic missiles: Khorramshahr-4, Kheibar, and Fattah hypersonic missiles are now being directed at US military installations.
  • From degradation to destruction: The target set has shifted from radar dishes and electronic systems to runways, hangars, command centers, and fuel storage—the permanent infrastructure that defines a base's existence.
  • Unified target bank: US bases in the Gulf and Israeli military installations are now treated as a single, integrated target set. Ramat David Air Base, Al-Udeid in Qatar, and bases in Kuwait and Bahrain are all on the same target list.

Ben Gurion Airport: The Strategic Chokepoint​

One of the most consequential strikes in Phase 2 has been directed at Ben Gurion International Airport. The targeting logic is both military and strategic: Ben Gurion's military wing serves as a critical logistics hub for the Israeli Air Force, and the airport itself is the only viable node for large-scale evacuation operations.

According to Israeli sources, over 2,000 residents have already been evacuated from Tel Aviv alone, with the number expected to grow. Approximately 5,000 Israeli nationals who had gathered in the UAE were preparing for an air bridge back to Israel via Ben Gurion. The strikes on the airport have disrupted this evacuation corridor, forcing a rerouting of operations.

The IRGC's intent is clear: take Ben Gurion's military wing permanently offline, exactly as Ramat David Air Base has been targeted for the same purpose. If both installations are rendered inoperable, Israel's capacity to sustain air operations and manage civilian evacuations simultaneously becomes severely constrained.

The Gulf States' Financial and Strategic Trap​

The economic dimension of Phase 2 is as critical as the military one. The United States has effectively demanded that Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states finance the war effort. This decision shifts the financial burden from the already-strained American and Israeli defense budgets onto Gulf economies that are themselves under severe pressure.

The economic clock is ticking for every party in the coalition. The longer the conflict continues, the more unsustainable the financing becomes—and Iran's strategy of graduated escalation is designed precisely to exploit this vulnerability.

Qatar's Al-Udeid Dilemma​

Al-Udeid Air Base in Qatar remains one of the most critical command and control nodes for American operations in the region. The Qatari authorities face an impossible dilemma: they want to protect the base's physical infrastructure—which represents billions in investment—while that same infrastructure is being used as a primary hub for Phase 2 operations against Iran.

This has created a direct crisis between Doha and Tehran. Iran views Al-Udeid's continued operational role as making Qatar a legitimate combatant in the conflict. The Qatari government argues it is providing only defensive support, but the operational reality—with Al-Udeid serving as a key command center for offensive strikes—makes that distinction increasingly untenable.

Kuwait and Bahrain: Maximum Exposure​

Among the Gulf bases, Kuwait and Bahrain are absorbing the heaviest pressure. The American military presence in Kuwait is being subjected to sustained attrition strikes, with large-scale drone swarms preceding the heavy missile waves. Bahrain, home to the US Fifth Fleet headquarters, has already been struck in Phase 1 and now faces infrastructure-level targeting in Phase 2.

The sequential escalation follows a clear pattern:

  1. Initial wave: Drone swarms against Kuwaiti bases to saturate air defenses.
  2. Follow-up: Heavy ballistic missile strikes targeting permanent infrastructure across all Gulf installations.
  3. Sustained pressure: Continued missile salvos designed to prevent repair and reconstitution of damaged facilities.

British, French, and Ukrainian air defense reinforcements deployed to Gulf states have proven insufficient against the new generation of Iranian missiles, which are specifically designed to penetrate layered defense systems.

The Israeli Air Force's Expanding Front​

Simultaneously, Israel's air operations are expanding into Lebanon, creating a multi-front resource drain. The campaign has followed a pattern of incremental escalation:

  1. Beirut Embassy: Initial plans to strike near the Iranian diplomatic presence were abandoned.
  2. Southern Suburbs (Dahieh): Direct strikes on Hezbollah infrastructure, framed as targeting IRGC presence.
  3. Bekaa Valley: Expansion of the strike zone deep into Lebanese territory.

Israeli ground forces have suffered casualties in these engagements, with at least eight soldiers reported as having sustained serious to critical injuries. This expanding front diverts resources from the primary campaign against Iran while simultaneously generating additional political and humanitarian complications.

The Attrition Equation: Iran's Strategic Patience​

The fundamental miscalculation by American and Israeli planners was the assumption that Iran would launch its entire missile inventory in a single massive salvo of 600-700 missiles, depleting over 50% of its reserves and creating an opportunity for a decisive follow-up strike.

Instead, Iran has adopted a strategy of graduated escalation—methodically increasing the intensity and expanding the target set while preserving its strategic reserves. This approach forces the coalition into a war of attrition that it cannot financially or operationally sustain:

  • Every day costs the coalition approximately $5.5 billion combined.
  • Gulf reserves have a 15-22 day operational window.
  • Iran's missile production capacity continues to operate, partially offsetting combat expenditures.
  • The IRGC explicitly states that "the initiative remains in our hands"—a direct response to the American claim of operational dominance.

Conclusion: Destruction Replaces Degradation​

Phase 2 represents a qualitative transformation in the conflict. The Iranian shift from degrading auxiliary systems to destroying permanent base infrastructure signals that the IRGC has abandoned restraint in its targeting doctrine. American bases in the Gulf are no longer being temporarily disabled—they are being systematically destroyed.

The financial burden, the expanding target set, and the graduated escalation strategy create an attrition dynamic that favors the defender. As the coalition burns through approximately $5.5 billion per day with a finite Gulf reserves window, the strategic question is no longer whether Phase 2 will succeed, but how long the coalition can sustain it before the economics force a political resolution.
