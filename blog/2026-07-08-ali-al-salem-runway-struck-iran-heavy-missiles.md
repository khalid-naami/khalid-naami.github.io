# Ali Al Salem Struck: Iran's Heavy Missiles in Kuwait

For the first time in this conflict, Iran has transitioned from standard ballistic missile strikes to heavy ballistic missile bombardment, and the target was the nerve center of American air power in the Gulf: Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait. The strike destroyed one of the base's two runways, immediately cutting US air operations capacity by 50% and marking a qualitative escalation that redefines the operational calculus for the entire theater.

This is not a harassing strike. This is a deliberate attempt to permanently remove Ali Al Salem from the order of battle.

## The Strike: From Ballistic to Heavy Ballistic

The Iranian attack on Ali Al Salem represents a fundamental escalation in weapons employment. Prior strikes against American installations in the Gulf used standard ballistic missiles and drone swarms—weapons designed to damage equipment, disable radars, and degrade electronic systems. The logic was disruption, not destruction.

That logic has now been abandoned. The IRGC deployed heavy ballistic missiles against Ali Al Salem with a single objective: destroy the physical infrastructure that makes the base operational. The primary target was the runway complex itself.

Ali Al Salem sits 144 meters above sea level and features two asphalt-and-concrete runways. The primary runway measures approximately 2,989 meters by 45 meters—a standard configuration for high-performance military aircraft operations. The strike destroyed this runway, reducing the base's operational capacity by half in a single salvo.

The second runway remains intact, but the precedent has been set. A follow-up strike targeting the remaining runway would take Ali Al Salem entirely offline. The base now operates in a precarious cycle: partial operations on the surviving runway, under the constant threat of a second strike that would deliver a complete shutdown.

## Why Ali Al Salem Matters: The Hub of American Air Power

Ali Al Salem is not merely another American installation in the Gulf. It is the principal center of US Air Force operations in the region, and its destruction would represent the single most consequential blow to American force projection capability since the conflict began.

The base serves as the permanent home of the **386th Air Expeditionary Wing**, which functions as:
- The primary air transit hub and gateway for combat support to Joint and Coalition forces.
- The main staging point for reconnaissance and surveillance operations across the theater.
- The coordination center for combat air support, medical evacuation, and combat search and rescue (CSAR).

Under the 386th AEW umbrella, several critical groups operate from Ali Al Salem:
- **386th Expeditionary Reconnaissance Group**: Aerial intelligence collection.
- **387th Expeditionary Reconnaissance Group**: Extended surveillance operations.
- **407th Expeditionary Reconnaissance Group**: Specialized reconnaissance missions.

Additionally, **the Ninth Air Force** maintains its effective headquarters at Ali Al Salem, making the base the command node for all US Air Force operations in the Central Command area of responsibility.

## The MQ-9 Reaper Factor

Perhaps the most strategically significant aspect of Ali Al Salem's operational profile is its role as the primary launch point for **MQ-9 Reaper** unmanned aerial vehicles. The MQ-9s that operate over Iranian airspace—conducting surveillance, target acquisition, and strike missions—originate from Kuwait.

This means that the drones being shot down over Iran, or conducting strike operations against Iranian targets, are overwhelmingly launching from Ali Al Salem. By striking the base's runway infrastructure, Iran is attacking the source of the drone operations that are directly supporting the bombing campaign against its territory.

The logic is direct and symmetrical: if the United States uses Kuwait-based drones to support strikes on Iranian missile cities, Iran will destroy the bases from which those drones operate.

## 13,500 American Troops: The Heaviest Footprint

Kuwait hosts the largest concentration of American military personnel in the Gulf theater. As of the current campaign, approximately **13,500 US troops** are stationed in Kuwait across multiple installations:

| Installation | Primary Function |
|---|---|
| **Ali Al Salem Air Base** | Main USAF operations hub, 386th AEW headquarters |
| **Camp Arifjan** | US Army installation with Air Force support units |
| **Ahmad Al Jaber Air Base** | Secondary air base hosting US air units and equipment |

This concentration of forces is a legacy of the 1991 Gulf War and the subsequent long-term strategic defense cooperation agreement between Kuwait and the United States. Since Operation Desert Storm, Kuwait has served as the primary staging ground for American military operations in the region—a role that has now made it the most exposed American position in the current conflict.

More American soldiers have been killed and wounded in Kuwait than in any other Gulf state during this campaign. The heavy troop presence, combined with the forward-deployed nature of the installations, makes Kuwait the epicenter of American vulnerability.

## The Strategic Defense Partnership: Since 1991

The US-Kuwait defense relationship is the deepest and most institutionally embedded partnership in the Gulf. Under the Long-Term Strategic Defense Cooperation Agreement, Kuwaiti bases have been integrated into American force structure to a degree unmatched by any other GCC state.

This integration was designed for power projection—enabling the United States to rapidly deploy and sustain forces across the Middle East. In the current crisis, however, that deep integration has become a strategic liability. The bases were configured for offensive operations against regional adversaries, not for defense against a state-level ballistic missile campaign directed at their permanent infrastructure.

The air defense systems protecting Kuwaiti installations were designed to handle shorter-range threats—rockets, mortars, and cruise missiles. Heavy ballistic missiles carrying warheads exceeding one ton present a threat category that the existing defensive architecture was not built to counter.

## The Repair-and-Strike Cycle

The destruction of one runway at Ali Al Salem creates a tactical dilemma that extends far beyond the immediate operational disruption. The base now faces what military planners call a "repair-and-strike cycle"—a pattern where damaged infrastructure is repaired, only to be struck again before full operations can resume.

This cycle is extraordinarily resource-intensive for the defending side:
- **Runway repair** requires specialized engineering teams, materials, and heavy equipment—all of which must be brought in under the threat of follow-up strikes.
- **Force protection** demands that defensive assets be concentrated around repair operations, drawing resources away from other installations.
- **Operational tempo** drops precipitously as commanders must plan around uncertain runway availability.

For the attacking side, the cycle is far less costly. A single heavy ballistic missile salvo can undo days or weeks of repair work in minutes. The attacker maintains the initiative, choosing when and where to strike, while the defender is locked into a reactive posture.

## The Heavy Bomber vs. Heavy Missile Equation

The escalation at Ali Al Salem crystallizes a symmetry that defines Phase 2 of the conflict: **heavy bombers against heavy missiles**.

The United States has deployed its most capable strategic bombers—the B-2 Spirit, B-52 Stratofortress, and B-21 Raider—to strike Iranian targets with massive ordnance. Iran has responded with its heaviest ballistic missiles to strike the bases from which those bomber operations are supported.

This creates a direct exchange ratio that neither side can sustain indefinitely:
- Every B-2 sortie against an Iranian missile city requires functional runways, fuel depots, and maintenance facilities at Gulf bases.
- Every Iranian heavy missile strike against those bases degrades the ability to generate the next B-2 sortie.
- The cost asymmetry favors the missile: a heavy ballistic missile costs a fraction of the infrastructure it destroys, while repairing a runway costs far more than the missile that cratered it.

## The 50% Reduction and What Comes Next

The immediate impact of the Ali Al Salem strike is quantifiable: a **50% reduction** in air operations capacity. With one runway destroyed and one remaining, the base can continue limited operations—but at half capacity, with degraded surge capability, and under the perpetual threat of a second strike.

The implications cascade through the entire theater:
- MQ-9 Reaper sorties over Iran are reduced.
- Air transit capacity for personnel and equipment is halved.
- Combat search and rescue response times increase.
- Medical evacuation capability is degraded.
- Reconnaissance flight schedules are compressed.

If the second runway is struck, Ali Al Salem goes completely dark. The 386th Air Expeditionary Wing would need to relocate—an operation measured in weeks, not hours—and the alternative basing options in the Gulf are themselves under missile threat.

## Conclusion: The Base That Cannot Fall

Ali Al Salem Air Base is the single most important American air installation in the Gulf theater. Its partial disabling represents a strategic achievement for the IRGC and a crisis for US Central Command. The transition from standard ballistic strikes to heavy missile bombardment signals that Iran has committed to a destruction doctrine—not disruption, not degradation, but the physical elimination of American basing infrastructure.

The 50% capacity reduction is the beginning, not the end. The repair-and-strike cycle ensures that Ali Al Salem will operate in a degraded state for the foreseeable future, and the 13,500 American troops in Kuwait now face the reality that their primary operational hub is no longer secure.

The heavy bomber versus heavy missile equation has arrived at its logical conclusion: neither side can project force without absorbing punishing retaliatory strikes against the infrastructure that enables that projection.
