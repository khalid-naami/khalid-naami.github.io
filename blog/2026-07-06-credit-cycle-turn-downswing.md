# The Credit Cycle: Where Are We in the Downswing?

What is a credit cycle and where are we in it? These are the questions increasingly coming up as stress ripples across the credit markets. 

Most of the noise is concentrated in private credit, where the expansion was loudest. But to understand the true macroeconomic picture, you have to think much more broadly.

## The Self-Reinforcing Nature of Lending Booms

A credit cycle is simply the expansion and contraction of lending. At its heart, however, it is a cycle of trust. Lenders, borrowers, and investors act in herds, reinforcing trends until reality forces a reversal. 

During the upswing, credit becomes easier a little at a time:
*   Lenders compete for deals by loosening loan covenants.
*   Borrowers secure better terms and higher leverage.
*   Investors accept lower yields for higher credit risk.
*   Collateral values rise, and defaults remain low.

Because defaults stay low during the boom, everyone concludes that lending standards were appropriate. Hyman Minsky described this dynamic decades ago: *stability breeds instability*. The longer conditions look calm, the more risk participants take because calm conditions make risk look smaller than it actually is. 

Bad lending doesn't look bad when the loan is made. It looks bad later, when the assumptions behind it—like stable interest rates and constant earnings growth—are proven false.

## The Four Stages of the Credit Cycle

Every credit cycle, regardless of the historical era, moves through four distinct phases:

### Stage 1: Repair
After a major bust, lenders are cautious and investors nurse their losses. Credit is available only to the strongest borrowers. basic economic survival and scar tissue dictate the market.

### Stage 2: Expansion
As the economy improves, defaults fall. Yield-starved investors return, and lenders find that cautious post-bust loans are performing well. Credit availability improves, and spreads begin to tighten.

### Stage 3: Excess
Too much capital begins chasing too few quality loans. Competition forces lenders to compromise on covenants. Debt structures become complex—showing up as CLOs, payment-in-kind (PIK) interest, and optimistic model-based valuations.

### Stage 4: The Turn
Growth slows, a specific sector faces stress, or the refinancing window begins to close. The turn starts quietly. At first, problems are dismissed as isolated incidents. But soon, the same loose assumptions are exposed across the entire system, and the cycle flips into a downswing.

## Why the Fed Doesn't Control the Cycle

A common textbook misconception is that the Federal Reserve controls the credit cycle via interest rate policy. History suggests otherwise. 

For instance, the Fed raised rates steadily from 2004 to 2006. Yet, the worst subprime mortgage excesses occurred during that exact period. The credit machine was not driven by the Fed's overnight rate; it was driven by global demand for yield, securitization structures, and rising collateral values. 

Similarly, the aggressive rate hikes of 2022 and 2023 did not immediately end the recent private credit boom. Instead, the cycle kept expanding as funds delayed defaults by modifying terms or allowing borrowers to pay interest with more debt (PIK). 

The turn only materialized when real economic cracks appeared in the labor market, exposing the structural slowdown we explored in our analysis of [home sales plunging to 2010 levels](/blog/home-sales-plunging-2010-levels) and [rising bond yields](/blog/bond-yields-rise-fed-policy-mistake).

## The Shadow Banking Center of Gravity

Every credit cycle has a center of gravity. In 2008, it was subprime mortgages held by commercial banks. Today, it is private credit issued by non-bank financial institutions (shadow banks).

Private credit ballooned post-2008 by offering a way for private equity sponsors to secure financing without public market disclosure. But the structural weaknesses of this system are now surfacing:
1.  **Illiquidity**: The underlying loans cannot be easily sold.
2.  **Model-Based Valuations**: Marks are based on internal models, not market transactions.
3.  **Redemption Pressures**: Investors are asking for their cash back, forcing major funds (including giants like Blackstone) to restrict withdrawals.
4.  **BDC Discounts**: Publicly traded Business Development Companies (BDCs) are trading below net asset value (NAV), signaling that the market does not trust the official marks.

---

## Conclusion: Navigating the Downswing

We have transitioned past the peak of the cycle. We are currently in the downswing, where the primary concern has shifted from *"how do I get more yield"* to *"how do I get my cash back"*. 

As refinancing costs remain high and corporate earnings feel the pinch of demand destruction, the private credit downswing will continue to pull on the rest of the financial system. For macro observers, tracking these subtle shifts in lending standards, credit availability, and investor redemptions is crucial to understanding what lies ahead.
