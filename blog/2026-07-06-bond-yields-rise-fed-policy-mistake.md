# Bond Yields Rise: Why the Fed Is Making a Policy Mistake

Bond yields rose sharply on Friday, but why? The mainstream narrative is that strong economic growth and sticky inflation are forcing the Federal Reserve to hike interest rates. 

But that is only half true. The Fed might indeed be gearing up to hike rates, but the underlying reason is not a booming economy or runaway inflation. Both the financial markets and American consumers agree on this.

## The Inflation Myth vs. The Policy Mistake

While nominal interest rates have drifted higher, inflation expectations in the Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) market have dropped to their lowest levels since the start of the conflict. The market is not pricing in a structural return of inflation. Instead, it is signaling that the Federal Reserve is preparing to make a classic policy error: panicking over oil prices and hiking rates directly into a weakening economy.

American consumers completely share this outlook. The latest Survey of Consumer Expectations from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York shows no sign of unanchored inflation psychology. Short-term inflation expectations are moving down, longer-term expectations remain anchored, credit conditions are deteriorating, and households are increasingly worried about their jobs.

Mainstream commentators are paying attention to lagging jobs data, but the underlying forward-looking indicators tell a very different story. Central bankers are preparing to react to short-term energy shocks, threatening to repeat the same demand-destruction policy errors of the past.

## What is the TIPS Market Actually Telling Us?

You rarely hear the mainstream media discuss the Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) market, even though it is the largest market explicitly dedicated to pricing inflation expectations. 

TIPS principal adjusts with the Consumer Price Index (CPI). The spread between a nominal Treasury yield and the real yield on a TIPS of the same maturity is the "break-even inflation rate." If the bond market were genuinely concerned about sustained, runaway inflation, we would see break-evens surge.

Instead, the opposite is happening:

*   **Underwhelming Levels**: The 5-year break-even previously ticked up toward 270 basis points. However, this is far below the 325–350 basis points seen during the broader supply shock of 2022.
*   **Recent Decline**: Even as oil and gasoline prices remain elevated due to the ongoing conflict and tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, the 5-year break-even rate recently fell back to around 248 basis points.
*   **Yield Curve Inversion**: Shorter-term break-evens remain higher than longer-term ones. This curve inversion demonstrates that the market views the current situation as a temporary energy-led CPI impulse, not embedded inflation.

## Consumer Psychology Refuses to Unanchor

The Federal Reserve's own consumer surveys contradict the hawkish rhetoric of its governors. The New York Fed's May Survey of Consumer Expectations shows that:

*   **Near-Term Inflation Expectations**: Median one-year inflation expectations decreased by a tenth of a percentage point to **3.5%**.
*   **Medium-Term Expectations**: Three-year and five-year expectations remained unchanged at **3.0%**, right around their long-term historical averages.
*   **Credit Strains**: Perceptions of credit availability deteriorated, with households reporting that credit is becoming harder to access.
*   **Labor Market Worry**: The perceived probability of finding a job if one's current job is lost fell by 2.3 percentage points to **43.7%**—the lowest level since December 2025. Meanwhile, the probability of losing a job rose to **15.1%**.

Americans are not worried about a wage-price spiral. They are worried about keeping their jobs and accessing credit.

## The Two-Year Yield: Hedging Against Fed Panic

If inflation is not the driver, why did the nominal 2-year Treasury yield rise so sharply? 

The 2-year Treasury is highly sensitive to short-term Federal Reserve policy. Traders are selling the 2-year not because they expect inflation to spiral, but to hedge against Fed panic. They know from history that central bankers often react to short-term, supply-driven headline CPI spikes with rate hikes.

Meanwhile, other parts of the money market are pricing in a very different reality. Treasury bill yields and term SOFR futures are hedging for economic weakness, pricing in eventual rate cuts as credit conditions tighten. 

The market is simultaneously pricing two risks:
1.  **Near-Term**: The Fed panics over oil prices and hikes rates (hedged by selling the 2-year Treasury).
2.  **Medium-Term**: The rate hikes accelerate demand destruction, forcing the Fed to pivot and cut rates (hedged in the SOFR futures curve).

## Conclusion: Another Trichet Moment

This macro setup strongly resembles 2008 and 2011, when Jean-Claude Trichet famously hiked rates at the European Central Bank. Trichet panicked over oil-driven inflation indicators right as the global economy was fracturing. 

High oil prices are not a sign of economic strength; they act as a tax on the consumer. When gas prices spike, households immediately cut discretionary spending. This leads to demand destruction, which resolves the price pressures on its own. 

If policy makers mistake a supply-side energy shock for broad-based inflation, they risk hiking rates directly into a recessionary trap. Both the bond market and consumers have read the indicators—it's time for the Fed to do the same.
